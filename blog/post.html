From USPTO to Freelance: What Being a Patent Examiner Taught Me About Software

← All posts

Most software engineers never have to think about patents. The legal side of the industry feels distant — something for lawyers and executives to worry about. I used to feel the same way.

Then I became a patent examiner at the USPTO, and everything changed.

What examiners actually do

The job is deceptively technical. Every application that lands on your desk is a puzzle: the applicant is claiming they invented something new. Your job is to figure out if they actually did — by digging through prior art, comparing claims line by line, and writing detailed rejections or allowances with legal backing.

For software applications, that means reading thousands of pages of code descriptions, flowcharts, and implementation details. You get surprisingly good at understanding systems quickly.

The lens it gave me

Spending years asking "what makes this novel?" fundamentally changed how I think about software. In engineering, you're often focused on making something work. At the USPTO, you're forced to ask whether it's actually new — and why.

That habit transfers back beautifully to engineering and freelancing. Before I build something, I now naturally ask: has this problem been solved? If so, how? What's genuinely different about this approach? It makes me a better architect and a better advisor.

What it means for my freelance work

My clients sometimes have ideas that touch on patentable territory — intentionally or not. Having a background at the USPTO means I can have conversations that most freelance engineers simply can't. I can't give legal advice, but I can help teams understand the landscape and know when to talk to an actual patent attorney.

It's an unusual combination. And increasingly, I think it's a valuable one.
